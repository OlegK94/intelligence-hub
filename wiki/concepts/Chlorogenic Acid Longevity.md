# Chlorogenic Acid (CGA) — Longevity Bioactive in Coffee

## Overview

**Chlorogenic acid (CGA)** is a polyphenol of the hydroxycinnamic acid family and the primary longevity-relevant bioactive compound in coffee. The most bioactive forms are **5-Caffeoylquinic acid (5-CQA)** and **3,4-Dicaffeoylquinic acid (3,4-diCQA)**. Robusta coffee contains significantly more CGA than Arabica — making it the preferred coffee base for functional/longevity products.

## Content by Coffee Type

| Source | CGA Content (% TG, Raw Bean) |
|---|---|
| **Robusta** (*C. canephora*) | **7–12%** |
| **Arabica** (*C. arabica*) | 4–8% |
| Espresso extract (Robusta, mg/100ml) | 150–300 mg |
| Espresso extract (Arabica, mg/100ml) | 80–180 mg |

*References: Clarke & Vitzthum (2001); Farah (2012)*

Robusta contains **up to 2× more CGA** than Arabica in the raw bean.

## Mechanisms of Action

| Mechanism | Detail |
|---|---|
| **AMPK Activation** | Activates AMP-activated protein kinase — same pathway as Metformin and caloric restriction; increases cellular energy efficiency and autophagy capacity |
| **Insulin Sensitivity** | Inhibits glucose-6-phosphatase in the liver → reduces postprandial glucose spikes (Johnston et al., 2003; Thom, 2007) |
| **Antioxidant Capacity** | Robusta extract ORAC value ~30% higher than Arabica extract at equivalent concentration |
| **Anti-inflammatory** | Inhibits NF-κB signaling pathway; reduces IL-6 and TNF-α in in-vitro models |

## Processing Losses — Roasting

CGA degrades significantly during roasting. This is the primary argument for **Light/Medium-Light roasting** of longevity coffee:

| Röstgrad | CGA-Erhalt | CGA-Verlust |
|---|---|---|
| Light Roast (210–220°C) | **45–55%** | 45–54% |
| Medium Roast (220–230°C) | 35–45% | ~60% |
| Dark Roast (230–250°C+) | <15% | >85–100% |

*Source: Farah et al. (2005), J. Agric. Food Chem.; SCA News (2014); Int. J. Food Sci. Technol. (2025)*

Key finding: Significant CGA degradation begins at 220°C; the process is non-linear.

## Processing Losses — Instant Extraction

After roasting, the extraction method further affects CGA content:

| Method | CGA Retention (of post-roast content) |
|---|---|
| Spray-Dried | 50–65% |
| **Freeze-Dried** | **70–85%** |
| Spray-Freeze-Dried | 80–85% |
| Cold Brew Freeze-Dried | 80–90% |

Freeze-Dried is the recommended method for maximum CGA retention while maintaining commercial viability.

## CGA in the Performance Coffee Sachet

**For the [[Performance Coffee Brand]] 2g Freeze-Dried Sachet (60/40 Robusta/Arabica):**

| Processing Stage | CGA Calculation |
|---|---|
| Raw coffee (2g, 60/40 blend) | ~136 mg CGA |
| After Medium-Light roast (×0.50) | ~68 mg |
| After Freeze-Drying + extraction (×0.75 × extraction yield ~60%) | **~25–45 mg/sachet** |

This is below the clinically relevant dosage for metabolic effects (**200–400 mg/day**) — suggesting multiple sachets per day or CGA supplementation from additional sources may be needed for full clinical effect. However, even 25–45 mg/sachet contributes meaningfully when combined with daily habitual coffee consumption.

## Clinical Relevance

- **Metabolic effects:** 200–400 mg/day in clinical studies for insulin sensitivity and glycemic control
- **AMPK activation:** Consistent with Metformin's mechanism — CGA is a natural AMPK activator
- **Longevity signaling:** AMPK activation → increased autophagy → cellular longevity pathway
